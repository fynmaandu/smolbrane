Let us dive into it from the perspective of a relativist and motivate it instead of defining it outright as a mathematician:

Consider something called a celestial sphere, which contains all the events you can see at one instant of time (in principle - can't do anything if you have myopia, yikes) This collection of events (from whom the light ray originated) forms a sphere in the past - this can be understood by the norm of null vector:

$t^2$ = constant = $x^2 + y^2 + z^2$ [This is our good old equation for a sphere]

Now that we have a sphere, it's better to use 2 new coordinates instead of restrictions on [x,y,z]; We could choose to use [θ,Φ] coordinates or take inspiration from complex analysis and make use of the stereographic coordinates [$\zeta,\bar{\zeta}$]. In the complex coordinates, we will have

$\zeta = {}^{(x+iy)}\!\!/\!_{(1-z)}$

and in the spherical coordinates we have

x = sin(θ)cos(Φ), y =sin(θ)sin(Φ), z = cos(θ)

To understand why one of the representations might be better than the other, let us look at how Lorentz transforms affect these null directions. As we are aware that Lorentz respects the causal relations, meaning any Lorentz transformation becomes some transformation (possibly with a new outlook) of the coordinates [$\zeta,\bar{\zeta}$] or [θ,Φ].(All we care about as of now are the null directions and not the lengths)

Further simplification that we plan on doing is to use $\zeta = {}^{\chi}\!\!/\!_{\eta}$ so that we don't have to deal with $\infty$ for the north pole. By this choice, we've entered the realm of $\mathbb{CP}$ because the coordinates $(\chi,\eta)=(\lambda\chi,\lambda\eta)\forall\lambda$. Complete Mathematical Picture

We define something called a "spinor space" using a complex 2-d vector space $\mathbb{S}$ endowed with a fixed alternating tensor $\epsilon_{AB}$. This spinor space is connected to the Lorentz vector space, and it also gives the Lorentzian metric of the spacetime using something called "principal null vectors"(will make it precise later) Spinor fields have an almost one-to-one relation to tensor fields; any tensorial quantity that we wrote in GR before can now be written in terms of multiple spinor fields. But with a small caveat that not all spacetimes admit a spinor field - for that, we need to check something called "Čech cohomology" of the manifold, which allows us to stitch together the $\epsilon_{AB}$ and the spinor field $\mathbb{S}$, which can be constructed on individual charts. (basically a sheaf theory mechanism).

    I'd like to add a remark that not all spinorial descriptions can be translated into tensors; for example, the description of a spin 1/2 field or Witten's proof of the positive energy theorem.
So one may ask why there is a lack of a proper one-to-one map between these geometric languages?
